Motion Experience

2022

At Motion Experience, an immersive museum in Eindhoven focused on light, projection, and spatial storytelling, I worked as an intern on several interactive installations. My tasks ranged from designing and implementing control systems to creating animations and experimenting with audience-triggered interactions. This internship allowed me to deepen my understanding of how technology and scenography intersect. I explored tools like VJ software and sensor-based inputs to enhance how people move through and engage with space. It was a hands-on opportunity to approach immersive experience design from both an aesthetic and a technical angle, and it confirmed my interest in working on large-scale, multi-sensory environments. 5.Motion Experience Date: 2022 Materials: Light, Projection, Product design This site-specific intervention took place at Tuinvereniging Streven naar Verbetering in Rotterdam, for Rotterdam Architecture Month. On arrival, I noticed a striking contradiction: a tall, closed fence protecting the space, while just behind it stood a sign reading “Everybody Welcome.” This tension—between invitation and exclusion—became the core of the work. I recreated a section of a nearby prison wall and designed posters inspired by local signage. Each poster subtly rephrased or questioned the garden association’s messaging, playing with the visual codes of the neighbor hood. Over four hours, I performed as a worker, pasting these posters onto the wall in layers. The act was both repetitive and disruptive, gradually building a visual noise that unsettled the clarity of the original message. The performance unfolded slowly, allowing space for casual conversations with passersby, some of whom lived nearby or had a connection to the garden. Their questions and reactions became part of the work—turning the piece into a kind of local scenography, where the environment, community, and performer continuously shaped each other. This work asks: What does it mean to be open, and who decides that openness? How do fences, signs, and repetition subtly guide behavior and perception? And how can we reimagine community spaces in a way that doesn’t sacrifice safety for inclusion, or vice versa?Iedereen welkom m.u.v bestemmingsverkeer 6. Date: 2024 Dimensions: Site-specific, 6m wall + posters Materials: Wood, printed posters, tape, performance This ongoing project began with a simple mechanical curiosity: insert a coin, receive a brief glimpse. But the peepshwow soon became a tool for research—testing how framing, spectatorship, and subversion function across different contexts. I experimented with maquettes, site placement, light filters, and access mechanics, always circling around the same question: What do people expect to see when they look through a frame? And what happens when that expectation is broken? These experiments evolved into Biecht—a coin-operated setup where participants kneel and view the phrase “What did you expect to find?” Moments later, they are photographed, and a speculative, AI-generated reflection based on their image is printed on a receipt. This twist—the “and then” moment—transforms the viewer into the viewed, reframing the interaction from spectacle to self-confrontation. The peepshow has become a flexible scenographic device in my practice: part installation, part performer, part interface. It stages expectation and destabilizes it, exploring the boundaries between privacy and exposure, control and surrender. This piece is not just about looking—it’s about being caught looking, and realizing the system is looking back.7.Peepshow exploration Date: 2024–ongoing Dimensions: Variable Materials: Coin mechanism, custom housing, screens, filters, AI-generated text, camera, printer Blocked Throat is an installation that stages a breakdown. It begins with calm familiarity—a meditative display of radio wave-generated light patterns invites passive observation. After a few minutes, viewers are asked to give feedback. A microphone is placed prominently, offering a clear next step: speak. But when they do, their voice is captured and returned to them as fragmented, distorted noise. The work draws on principles from product design, where good systems guide users seamlessly from perception to action. I mimic this logic to build trust—only to rupture it. The microphone, an object that normally promises agency and clarity, be comes a site of failure. It doesn’t silence; it interrupts, rendering communication difficult, even absurd. This moment—the “And Then”—is the core of the piece. Viewers realize the system they trusted is unreliable. The breakdown is not a bug but the message. It forces participants to question not only what they’re experiencing, but how they expect systems to behave. Rather than providing answers, Blocked Throat creates friction. It critiques how pre dictability is engineered into our daily interactions—from digital platforms to public services—conditioning us to expect control and efficiency.
